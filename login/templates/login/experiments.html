ANALYSIS OF OILS- IODINE NUMBER- SAPONIFICATION VALUE -ACID NUMBER

ANALYSIS OF OILS- IODINE NUMBER- SAPONIFICATION VALUE -ACID NUMBER

This experiment helps determine the quality and composition of oils by measuring key parameters like iodine number, saponification value, and acid number. These values indicate the level of unsaturation, the molecular weight of fatty acids, and the presence of free acids, which are crucial for assessing oil purity and usability in industries like food, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals

DETERMINATION OF TOTAL CRUDE FAT / OIL BY SOXHLET’S METHOD

DETERMINATION OF TOTAL CRUDE FAT / OIL BY SOXHLET’S METHOD

This experiment is used to estimate the total lipid (fat/oil) content in a given sample using the Soxhlet extraction method. The process involves dissolving lipids in organic non-polar solvents like petroleum spirit, benzene, or hexane, followed by continuous heating and cooling for efficient extraction. The extracted crude fat/oil is then weighed to determine its percentage in the sample. This method is widely used in food, agriculture, and pharmaceutical industries to analyze the fat content in various biological materials.

ENZYME ASSAY- AMYLASE & INVERTASE

ENZYME ASSAY- AMYLASE & INVERTASE

This experiment measures the activity of two enzymes: amylase and invertase. Amylase breaks down starch into glucose and maltose, while invertase breaks down sucrose into glucose and fructose. The enzyme activity is determined by measuring the color change caused by the reaction products using a spectrophotometer. This helps understand how these enzymes work in breaking down sugars.

ESTIMATION OF GLUCOSE – DINITROSALICYLIC ACID

ESTIMATION OF GLUCOSE – DINITROSALICYLIC ACID

This experiment estimates the amount of glucose in a solution by using the dinitrosalicylic acid (DNS) method. Glucose, being a reducing sugar, reacts with DNS, reducing it and forming a red-colored product. This red color is measured at 570 nm using a spectrophotometer. The intensity of the color is directly proportional to the glucose concentration. A standard curve is plotted by using known glucose concentrations to calculate the glucose content in the unknown sample.

ESTIMATION OF PROTEIN – LOWRY’S METHOD

ESTIMATION OF PROTEIN – LOWRY’S METHOD

This experiment estimates the protein content in a sample using the Lowry’s method. The protein reacts with alkaline copper sulfate to form a complex, and then the Folin-Ciocalteau reagent is added, which reacts with the amino acids tyrosine and tryptophan in the protein, producing a blue color. The intensity of this blue color is proportional to the amount of protein in the sample, and it is measured at 660 nm. By comparing the sample's optical density with a standard curve, the protein concentration in the sample can be determined.

ESTIMATION OF REDUCING AND NON-REDUCING SUGAR

ESTIMATION OF REDUCING AND NON-REDUCING SUGAR

This experiment measures the amount of reducing and non-reducing sugars in a food sample.
1. **Reducing Sugars**: The reducing sugars react with Fehling's solution, causing a color change. The amount of Fehling's solution used is measured to calculate the reducing sugars in the sample.
2. **Non-reducing Sugars**: Non-reducing sugars (like sucrose) are first converted into reducing sugars by adding acid. Then, the total reducing sugars are measured using the same Fehling’s solution. The difference between total reducing sugars and reducing sugars gives the sucrose content.
In short, this experiment calculates the percentage of reducing sugars, total sugars, and sucrose in the sample.

ESTIMATION OF TOTAL FREE AMINO ACID – NINHYDRIN METHOD

ESTIMATION OF TOTAL FREE AMINO ACID – NINHYDRIN METHOD

This experiment estimates free amino acids in a sample using the Ninhydrin method. The sample is first extracted using ethanol, and then the amino acids react with Ninhydrin to form a purple color. The intensity of this color is measured at 570 nm using a colorimeter. A standard curve is prepared using known concentrations of leucine, and the free amino acid concentration in the unknown sample is determined by comparing its color intensity to the standard. The result is expressed as the percentage equivalence of leucine.

PREPARATION OF STANDARD SOLUTIONS

PREPARATION OF STANDARD SOLUTIONS

This experiment demonstrates the preparation, dilution, and standardization of sodium hydroxide (NaOH) solutions. It begins with accurately preparing a standard NaOH solution by dissolving a known weight of solid NaOH in water and transferring it to a volumetric flask. The solution is then diluted to a lower concentration. To standardize the NaOH solution, it is titrated with a standardized hydrochloric acid (HCl) solution, using thymol blue as an indicator to identify the endpoint of the reaction. The molarity of the NaOH solution is calculated through titration and dilution calculations.

QUANTIFICATION OF VITAMIN C

QUANTIFICATION OF VITAMIN C

This experiment is designed to quantify the amount of Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) in a biological sample, such as citrus fruit juice. The principle involves the reduction of 2,6-dichlorophenol indophenol (DCIP) dye by Vitamin C, turning the dye from blue to colorless. The endpoint of the titration is marked by the appearance of a pale pink color. A standard Vitamin C solution is prepared and titrated with the DCIP dye, and the volume of dye consumed is used to calculate the concentration of Vitamin C in the test sample. The result provides the amount of Vitamin C present in 100 grams of the fruit.

SEPERATION OF PLANT PIGMENTS BY COLOUMN CHROMATOGRAPHY

SEPERATION OF PLANT PIGMENTS BY COLOUMN CHROMATOGRAPHY

This experiment demonstrates the separation of plant pigments using column chromatography, a technique based on the differential adsorption of compounds onto a solid stationary phase (silica gel) and their selective elution using a mobile phase (a mixture of petroleum ether and acetone). The plant pigments are first extracted from fresh leaves and introduced into the chromatography column, where they interact with the silica gel. As the mobile phase flows through the column, different pigments move at varying rates due to their differing affinities for the stationary phase, leading to their separation into distinct colored bands. The separated pigments are then collected in different fractions, allowing for their individual analysis.
